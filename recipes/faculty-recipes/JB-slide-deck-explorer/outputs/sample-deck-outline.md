## Argument reconstruction (before slides)

- **Thesis:** What we owe a decision-subject is not just *explanation* but
  *answerability* — a standing from which she can contest the decision before an
  agent who can be moved by her reasons. ("Transparency without contestability
  leaves the decision-subject informed but powerless.")
- **Premise 1:** Explanation is backward-looking; respecting someone as a rational
  agent is forward-looking — it grants her a role in what happens next.
- **Premise 2:** Accountability requires an *addressable agent*; automated systems
  are built to remove any such addressee.
- **Premise 3:** The wrong is *relational*, not merely distributive — two systems
  with identical accuracy and distribution still differ if one permits appeal.
- **Objection:** Contestability is just slower, costlier explanation (appeals
  merely surface better explanations).
- **Reply:** That mistakes the *form* of contestation for its *point*; an appeal
  that only re-runs the model is a *counterfeit* of answerability, not an instance.
- **Upshot:** The design question is not only "can we explain the output?" but "to
  whom can the subject address her challenge, and can that agent change it?"

## Slide outline

### Slide 1 — Title
**Key line:** What We Owe Decision-Subjects Beyond Explanation
**Speaker notes:** Set the scene with a concrete case before the title lands — a
loan denial, an audit flag, a low transplant ranking. The person on the receiving
end is the "decision-subject." Today's claim: the standard remedy, explanation,
isn't enough.

### Slide 2 — The puzzle (hook, not roadmap)
**Key line:** When an algorithm decides against you, the standard demand is: *tell
me why.* Is that enough?
**Speaker notes:** Law and ethics have converged on transparency/explanation as
the fix. I want to grant that explanation is necessary and then show it leaves
something important out. Lead with the live question, not an outline of the talk.

### Slide 3 — Thesis
**Key line:** *"What we owe a decision-subject is not merely that the decision be
explained to her, but that it be answerable to her."*
**Speaker notes:** Quote exactly — this is the load-bearing sentence. "Answerable"
means: she has standing to challenge the decision and trigger reconsideration by
someone who can be held responsible. I call this the **duty of contestability**.
Transparency without it = informed but powerless, and that powerlessness is a
distinct wrong.

### Slide 4 — Premise 1 of 3
**Key line:** Explanation is backward-looking; respect is forward-looking.
**Speaker notes:** An explanation tells you what already happened — features,
weights, the threshold you missed. But treating someone as a rational agent grants
her a role in what happens *next*. The weather analogy: a perfect explanation of
the weather doesn't make the weather answerable to me. Understanding why you acted
doesn't exhaust our moral relationship; my standing to ask you to think again is
part of it.

### Slide 5 — Premise 2 of 3
**Key line:** Accountability requires an addressable agent — and automation is
built to remove one.
**Speaker notes:** To contest is to address a demand to *someone* who can receive
it, weigh it, and revise or justify. Automated systems distribute "the decision"
across a model, a dataset, a procurement contract, a deployment policy — no single
point where a human stands ready to answer. No addressee, no one to hear the
demand: the duty is violated even if every output is scrupulously explained.

### Slide 6 — Premise 3 of 3
**Key line:** The wrong is relational, not merely distributive.
**Speaker notes:** Suppose all you care about is accuracy and fair distribution.
Then picture two systems, identical in both, one allowing appeal to a responsible
officer and one not. The subjects under the second are wronged in a way the first
are not — *even though no one's outcome changes*. The wrong is in the relation: in
being placed where your reasons can't get a hearing. This is the slide that shows
the thesis isn't reducible to fairness metrics.

### Slide 7 — Where we are (one-slide roadmap)
**Key line:** Thesis + three premises -> now the strongest objection.
**Speaker notes:** Brief structural breath. Restate: answerability beats mere
explanation (thesis), because explanation is backward-looking (P1), accountability
needs an addressee (P2), and the wrong is relational (P3). Now I owe you the best
objection.

### Slide 8 — The objection
**Key line:** "Contestability is just slower, more expensive explanation."
**Speaker notes:** Steelman it. The critic says contestability collapses into
explanation + an appeals process, and appeals just surface better explanations. If
an appeal merely re-runs the model with more data, nothing morally new is added —
we've only paid more for the same backward-looking good. State this as strongly as
its proponent would.

### Slide 9 — Reply (1): form vs. point
**Key line:** *"This mistakes the form of contestation for its point."*
**Speaker notes:** Exact quote. The point of an addressable agent isn't a better
explanation — it's to instantiate a *relationship of answerability*: to make it
the case that the subject's reasons can, in principle, move someone with authority
to revise.

### Slide 10 — Reply (2): the counterfeit test
**Key line:** An appeal that only re-runs the model is a *counterfeit* of
contestability, not an instance of it.
**Speaker notes:** It keeps the surface form ("you may appeal") while removing the
substance (no one whose mind can change). That we can build counterfeits doesn't
show the genuine article is empty — it shows why the duty must be specified as
*answerability*, not as *process*. Keep this on its own slide; it's the move that
defeats the objection.

### Slide 11 — Upshot for design
**Key line:** The question is not only "can we explain the output?" but "to whom
can the subject address her challenge, and can that agent change the decision?"
**Speaker notes:** Systems engineered to be perfectly explicable yet perfectly
unanswerable don't discharge the duty — they disguise its violation. Close on the
reframed design question; that's the portable takeaway for a mixed audience.

## Flattening watch
- **Premise 3 is the fragile one on slides.** Its force comes from holding two
  near-identical systems side by side; a single bullet loses that. Consider a
  simple two-column visual (System A: appeal / System B: no appeal — same accuracy,
  same outcomes) so the *relational* difference is seen, not just asserted.
- **The reply (slides 9–10) must stay two slides.** Compressing "form vs. point"
  and "the counterfeit test" into one bullet list collapses exactly the move that
  answers the objection — the worst possible place to flatten.
- **If this were a close-reading talk** (it isn't — it's a constructive argument),
  slides would be the wrong medium and a projected passage + handout would beat a
  deck. Flagged per the principles file so the judgment is explicit.
