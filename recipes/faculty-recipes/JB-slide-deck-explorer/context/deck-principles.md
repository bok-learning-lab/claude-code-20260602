# Slide-ifying a humanities argument *well*

Notes for turning argumentative prose into a deck that respects the reasoning.
The enemy is "bullet mush": slides that list topics ("Background," "Key Points,"
"Conclusions") and lose the chain of moves that *is* the paper.

## 1. One move per slide
A paper is a sequence of *moves*, not a list of *subjects*. Each slide should
carry exactly one move: a single premise, a single objection, a single reply.
If a slide is doing two jobs, split it. If two slides are doing one job, merge
them. The slide count should fall out of the argument's structure, not the other
way around.

## 2. Make the slide show *where you are* in the argument
The audience should always know which part of the argument they are looking at.
Label the structural role, not just the content:
- "Thesis," "Premise 1 of 3," "The objection," "Reply to the objection,"
  "What follows."
Signposting the move is more important than a clever title. A reader who loses
the thread of a humanities talk has lost everything.

## 3. The slide states the *claim*; the notes carry the *nuance*
Put **one sentence — the load-bearing claim** — on the slide, in the author's own
register where possible. Everything that makes it defensible (the qualification,
the example, the move that anticipates a worry) goes in the **speaker notes**.
The slide is a handhold; the notes are the argument. This is the single most
important habit for a close reader: it lets the deck be sparse without being
dumb.

## 4. Bullets are minimal and honest
If a slide truly has parts (e.g. three premises previewed at once), bullets are
fine — but each bullet must be a *claim*, not a topic word. "Explanation is
backward-looking" is a claim; "Explanation" is mush. Never pad to fill a slide.
A slide with one sentence and a lot of white space is a feature.

## 5. Preserve dialectic: objection and reply get their own slides
The most common flattening is dropping objections because they "complicate" the
slide. Don't. The objection slide should state the strongest version of the
worry; the reply slide should state the response. Keeping them adjacent and
distinct *is* the philosophy. A deck that only presents conclusions has thrown
away the part a scholar would defend.

## 6. Quotation over paraphrase for the pivotal lines
For the thesis and the key reply, prefer the author's actual sentence to a
paraphrase. Paraphrase is where nuance leaks out. A single exact quotation on a
slide, with attribution, often does more work than three bullets.

## 7. Open with the stakes, not the literature review
Lead with the thesis or the puzzle that motivates it. Audiences grant attention
for a live question, not for "here is the structure of my talk." The roadmap, if
any, is one slide and comes after the hook.

## When NOT to use slides at all
Be honest about this — JB asked the real question. Slides are a poor fit when:
- the argument turns on **close reading of a text** (the passage itself, shown
  and dwelt on, is the medium — a handout or a single projected quotation beats a
  deck);
- the talk is **dialogic / seminar-style**, where the value is in live
  back-and-forth and slides would freeze the discussion;
- the reasoning is **densely interdependent**, so that no clean decomposition
  into discrete moves survives without distortion.

In these cases the right deliverable may be a **handout, a single text slide, or
no slides at all**. Recommending against a deck is a legitimate — sometimes the
best — outcome of this workflow.
