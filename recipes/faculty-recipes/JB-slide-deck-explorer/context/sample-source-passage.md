## What We Owe Decision-Subjects Beyond Explanation

When an algorithm denies someone a loan, flags them for audit, or ranks them low
on a transplant list, the dominant response in both law and ethics has been to
demand *explanation*: the affected person should be told why the system decided
as it did. Call the person on the receiving end of such a decision a
**decision-subject**. I argue that explanation, though necessary, is not
sufficient — and that the prevailing focus on it obscures a distinct obligation
we owe decision-subjects, which I call the duty of **contestability**.

**Thesis.** What we owe a decision-subject is not merely that the decision be
*explained* to her, but that it be *answerable to her* — that she occupy a
standing from which she can challenge the decision and trigger a reconsideration
by an agent who can be held responsible. Transparency without contestability
leaves the decision-subject informed but powerless, and powerlessness of this
specific kind is a distinct wrong.

**First premise: explanation is backward-looking; respect is forward-looking.**
An explanation tells the decision-subject what *already* happened — the features,
the weights, the threshold she fell below. But to treat a person as a rational
agent rather than a mere object of administration is to grant her a role in what
happens *next*. A decision she cannot question is, from her standpoint, simply a
fact about the world, like the weather. Even a perfectly accurate explanation of
the weather does not make the weather answerable to me. The moral relationship we
have to one another is not exhausted by my understanding why you acted; it
includes my standing to ask you to think again.

**Second premise: accountability requires an addressable agent.** To contest a
decision is to address a demand to *someone* — someone who can receive the
challenge, weigh it, and either revise the decision or justify holding to it.
Automated systems are built precisely to remove such an addressee: the
"decision" is distributed across a model, a dataset, a procurement contract, and
a deployment policy, with no one point at which a human stands ready to answer.
Where there is no addressable agent, there is no one to whom the demand for
reconsideration can be made, and so the duty of contestability is violated even
if every output is scrupulously explained.

**Third premise: the wrong is relational, not merely distributive.** One might
think the only thing that matters is whether decisions are *accurate* and
*fairly distributed* — whether the right people get loans, audits, organs. But
consider two systems with identical accuracy and identical distributions, one of
which permits appeal to a responsible officer and one of which does not. The
decision-subjects under the second system are wronged in a way the first are not,
even though no one receives a different outcome. The wrong is in the *relation* —
in being placed in a standing where one's reasons cannot get a hearing.

**Objection: contestability is just a slower, more expensive explanation.** A
critic may reply that what I am calling contestability collapses into
explanation plus an appeals process, and appeals processes are themselves just
mechanisms for surfacing better explanations. If the appeal merely re-runs the
model with more data, nothing morally new has been added; we have only paid more
for the same backward-looking good.

**Reply.** This mistakes the *form* of contestation for its *point*. The point of
an addressable agent is not to generate a better explanation but to instantiate a
relationship of answerability — to make it the case that the decision-subject's
reasons can, in principle, *move* someone with the authority to revise. An appeals
process that merely re-runs the model is precisely a counterfeit of
contestability, not an instance of it: it preserves the surface form (one may
"appeal") while removing the substance (there is no one whose mind can be
changed). That we can build counterfeits does not show the genuine article is
empty; it shows why we must specify the duty in terms of answerability rather
than process.

**Conclusion.** We owe decision-subjects more than transparency. We owe them a
standing from which the decision is answerable — an addressable agent who can be
moved by their reasons. Systems engineered to be perfectly explicable yet
perfectly unanswerable do not discharge this duty; they disguise its violation.
The design question is therefore not only "can we explain the output?" but "to
whom can the subject address her challenge, and can that agent change the
decision?"
