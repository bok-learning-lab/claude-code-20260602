Student Simulator

Rehearse your microteaching mini-lesson against a simulated intro-chem student before you face a real one.

How it works

  1. Have your mini-lesson ready (outline or full plan).
  2. Pick a student persona below, and a level — level 1 makes the misconception transparent (good for a first rehearsal); level 2 wraps the same pattern in realistic social texture. Full profiles are in context/personas/level1/ and level2/.
  3. Open the system prompt prompts/01-student-simulator.md, paste in your lesson and the chosen persona, set the intensity, and start teaching.
  4. The simulated student stays in character and questions you for ~6–8 exchanges. Type DEBRIEF when you're done.
  5. Run prompts/02-debrief.md for coaching feedback.
  6. Hand in: your saved chat transcript + your lesson plan.

Choose a student

Five failure modes documented in the chemistry-education-research literature. Each works at level 1 or level 2, and transfers across general, organic, physical, and inorganic chemistry — not just the acid/base examples shown.

Procedure Follower algorithmic

Failure mode: treats procedures as explanations; a right answer feels like understanding. Steps are disconnected from meaning.

Asks "which formula?" before "what's happening?"; restates steps as if they explain.

Parts-to-Whole Projector emergence

Failure mode: attributes a system's emergent properties to its component atoms ("sodium is reactive, so…").

Reasons element → compound, skipping structure and interaction.

Single-Factor Reasoner one variable

Failure mode: reduces a multi-variable phenomenon to one dominant factor and applies it universally; exceptions feel like noise.

Asks which factor is "the main one"; destabilized by exceptions.

Everyday Language Student vocabulary

Failure mode: reasons from colloquial meanings of scientific terms — "spontaneous" = fast, "equilibrium" = stopped, "strong" = concentrated.

Can state the definition, then reason from the everyday version in the next breath.

Pattern Matcher surface similarity

Failure mode: categorizes problems by how they look versus prior examples, not by causal reasoning ("this has an OH, so it's basic, right?").

Naming the pattern functions as the explanation; surprised when look-alikes differ.
