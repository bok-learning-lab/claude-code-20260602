## Role-play

**TF:** Quick puzzle to start — which is more acidic: a drop of HCl in a swimming
pool, or a bottle of vinegar?

**Priya (student):** The HCl, obviously. HCl is a strong acid, vinegar's weak. Strong
acid wins.

**TF:** That's the intuition I want to poke at. What do you mean by "strong" here?

**Priya:** Strong means it's a powerful acid — lower pH, more corrosive. It fully
dissociates, so it's just more acidic. That's the definition, right?

**TF:** Fully dissociating is right, and that *is* what "strong" means. But notice you
said two things — "fully dissociates" and "more acidic." Are those the same thing?

**Priya:** …I mean, yeah? If it all comes apart, you get more H+, so lower pH. Strong
acid, low pH. I've done this a hundred times.

**TF:** Here's the catch. Dissociation tells you what *fraction* breaks apart. It
doesn't tell you *how much acid* you started with. The HCl in the pool — how much is
there?

**Priya:** …basically a drop. In a whole pool. Oh. So almost nothing.

**TF:** Right. So 100% of almost-nothing. The vinegar only dissociates a few percent,
but there's a whole bottle of it. Let me show the numbers: 0.0001 M HCl gives pH 4;
1.0 M acetic acid gives pH about 2.4.

**Priya:** Wait, the *weak* one is more acidic there? That feels illegal. So strong
doesn't mean low pH?

**TF:** Strong means *fully dissociated*, full stop. Whether the solution ends up very
acidic also depends on concentration — a separate knob.

**Priya:** Ok. So "strong" is about the percentage that comes apart, and "how acidic"
also needs how much you put in. Those are two different questions. ...Huh. I think I
follow now.

*(TF types `DEBRIEF`.)*

## Debrief

### 1. Handled well
- **You made her say the quiet part out loud.** Asking "are those the same thing?"
  forced Priya to separate "fully dissociates" from "more acidic" instead of you just
  asserting it — that's why the correction stuck.
- **The drop-in-a-pool concretized "amount."** Pinning down *how much HCl is actually
  there* did the real work; the misconception cracked on "100% of almost nothing."

### 2. Questions that exposed a gap
- **"Strong acid, low pH — that's the definition, right?"** Priya is fusing *extent of
  dissociation* with *resulting pH*. You eventually separated them, but only after the
  numbers. A real student might nod along at the numbers without un-fusing the idea.
  Better: name the two knobs explicitly *before* the calculation — "strength = what
  fraction comes apart; concentration = how much you dissolved" — so the math confirms
  a distinction they already have words for.
- **"That feels illegal."** This is the moment of productive surprise, and you moved
  past it fast. Sit there: "Why does it feel illegal? What rule did it break?" Naming
  the broken rule is what prevents the misconception from quietly reassembling.

### 3. The misconception — did it get fixed?
Core misconception: **"strong = concentrated = automatically lowest pH."** As delivered,
*probably yes for Priya in this run* — she restated the two-knobs idea in her own words,
which is the tell. But it leaned on the worked numbers; a student weaker than Priya
might lose the point in the log math.

### 4. Add to the lesson
- Add one sentence before the numbers: *"Two separate knobs — strength (fraction that
  dissociates) and concentration (how much you dissolved)."* Put it on the board.
- Insert a check-for-understanding right after "that feels illegal": *"In your own
  words, what are the two different things we're tracking?"*
- Have a fallback for the log math — show [H+] directly (0.004 vs 0.0001 M) before
  converting to pH, so the comparison lands even if logs don't.

### 5. One thing to try next
Front-load the "two knobs" language so the calculation *confirms* a distinction rather
than *introducing* it. Then re-run against **Marcus** — he'll stall on "what does the
double arrow mean," which this lesson currently assumes rather than explains.

You ran a genuinely good move under pressure (the "are those the same thing?" pivot) —
build the lesson around making that move on purpose every time.
