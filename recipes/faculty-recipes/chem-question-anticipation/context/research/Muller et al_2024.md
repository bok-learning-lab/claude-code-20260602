Conceptions
We apply the definition by Gilbert and who state that “conceptions’ be used to focus on the
personalized theorizing and hypothesizing of individuals” (p 69). They can be accessed
through actions of people, including speech and writing, but also nonverbal actions such as
gestures. Naturally, not all conceptions are correct or accurate in the sense that they agree
with current scientific thought (in the case of scientific conceptions). These incomplete or
inaccurate conceptions have been termed alternative conceptions,’ or misconcep- tions.'°
Often, they are accurate in the specific context in which they were integrated but are then
wrongfully generalized to other contexts. How learners’ conceptions evolve toward expert
knowledge, that is how conceptual change can occur, is theorized in two major lines of
research—theory change’! and knowledge-in-pieces.'* The former is not to be confused with
the similar term theory of change used in the field of evaluation to describe reforms in
education.'* Both lines assume that small knowledge structures are crucial to conceptual
change. Important for the presented work is the cross-domain mapping, which plays a role in
both lines of reasoning. This process is characterized by the assumption that new information
must be integrated into prior knowledge. The new information or target domain includes the
target concept that is taught. What is included in the source domain depends on the
theoretical framework that is applied. However, all of the mentioned accounts involve minimal
knowledge structures that are often experiential and embodied. In both frameworks,
misconceptions arise when (i) they are mapped inaccurately to the source domain or (ii) they
are not actually accessible to the learner.'* For example, Venville and Treagust’> designed a
study that introduced DNA metaphorically as a code, a metaphor that is commonly applied in
textbooks, such as, for example, “cells replicate their DNA [... and] decode the instructions
represented in the DNA” (ref 16, p 25). They found that whether this metaphor is productive
for learning depends strongly on the learner’s conception of code. For example, one of their
students potentially conceptualized code as a series of numbers and consequently did not
conceptualize DNA as a code but rather as having a code, which resulted in different,
potentially incorrect reasoning. For chemistry specifically, Talanquer'” has suggested a model
to classify and understand students’ misconceptions based on what he calls commonsense

---

chemistry. We argue that his framework aligns well with the conceptual change framework
described in this section. Students typically start their academic journey with more experience
in everyday activities and common sense reasoning, and less experience in the chemical
domain, especially due to the emergent nature of chemical knowledge structure, which can
result in misconcep- Therefore, these commonsense conceptions should be considered when
introducing new concepts. Students are not presented with new information in a vacuum, but
they will ground this information in prior experience or knowledge. Here, we are interested in
what chemical bond conceptions the students apply when they are first confronted with the
potential energy surface and, thereby, with new tools to argue about stability and reactivity.

requires energy, so does the formation of a chemical bond. Similar evidence was also found

concept of potential energy in the chemical domain, due to its prior introduction in multiple

Materials

systems while the latter responds in real-time. On the screen, the students saw a potential

interact with the molecular structure by dragging atoms across the screen and hence explore
different reaction mechanisms. The rest of the structure simultaneously relaxed according to

Procedure
The procedure of the problem-solving phase was almost identical for the first three studies.
They differ only in the qualitative data obtained. For study I, the students thought aloud during
the process, while in studies II and II, they answered open-ended questions in written form
directly on the instruction sheets. The quantitative data obtained in each study differed based
on the goals of each study, but as this does not concern our work here, we omit the details.
Consent was sought from all students before the start of the studies. After the first phase of
quantitative measures, the students performed the problem-solving phase. The problem to be
solved was the execution of a reaction, that is, the creation of a specific product from given
reactants. The experience started with rotation, zooming, and dragging and dropping tasks to
familiarize themselves with the Touch haptic device. After self-proclaimed proficiency, the
students moved on to the learning tasks. These three tasks consisted of (i) a single molecule
that they could manipulate as they pleased to explore the connection between energy change
and force and (ii) two nucleophilic substitutions with differing reactants. In addition to exploring
different reaction mechanisms, the students could also explore the effect of molecular charge
or visualize orbitals and electron density. After the tasks were finished, the students again
filled out questionnaires for the quantitative measures before receiving the instruction phase
(for studies II and III) or being finished (for study 1). All studies took between 90 and 120 min.
During the whole process, the instructor aimed to interrupt as little as possible. Still, there
were some technical issues, and the students sometimes struggled with the
three-dimensional navigation of the haptic device, with which the instructor then assisted. The
reader interested in the procedure in more detail may refer to Miiller and co-workers (2024).**

@ DISCUSSION
Thematic analysis of the data of three studies investigating the effect of haptic feedback on
learning quantum chemical concepts resulted in the common theme that chemical bonding is
conceptualized in various models. Here, we reanalyzed the data related to the chemical bond
and generated three themes that describe how students conceptualized the chemical bond in
this particular context. The results of this analysis are triangulated with a metaphor analysis of
interviews, which resulted in similar findings. In the following section, the reasons for
similarities and differences are discussed. We found that chemical bonding was viewed as an
event (“the chemical bond as rigid entity”) by 15/31 (3 first, 6 second, 6 fourth semester)
students and as a process (“the chemical bond related to energy”) by 19/31 (1, 8, 10) students
with 9 (1, 5, 3) students using both. The third theme “Orbitals as indicators of the presence or
stability of the bond” was found substantially less frequently (mentioned by only four (0, 2, 2)
students; see Figure 4). The event and process conceptions were not mutually exclusive and

Limitations
The data for this analysis were limited in the sense that conceptions of the chemical bond
were not the primary focus of a large part of the data collection and, hence, questions focused
on the connections between the energy graph and the simulation. In studies I-III, the students
were not explicitly asked about their interpretation of the visual representation of the
molecules. Accordingly, our data were limited to events in which the students were confused
or in disagreement with the visual representation and mentioned this on their question- naire
or in their think-aloud interview. Furthermore, all studies were framed as a testing of the
learning environment for future generations of chemistry students. We could therefore expect
certain distrust in the representations, including the presence of the chemical bond. While
here the chemical bond as a construct that is there or not is highly supported by the
visualization, future research should explore how other visualizations, such as the electron
density, influence the activated conceptions or how the students would reason about the
chemical bond without visual input at all. Finally, expert language seems to have an impact on
the metaphorical language employed by students. A systematic analysis of the former would
be of interest.
