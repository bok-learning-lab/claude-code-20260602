# Sample microteaching mini-lesson (TF submission)

- **TF:** (sample) A. Liang
- **Course:** Chem 11 (intro general chemistry), discussion section
- **Topic:** Strong vs. weak acids — why "strong" is not the same as "concentrated"
- **Length:** 8-minute microteaching segment
- **Audience:** First-year students who have seen pH and the formula
  `pH = -log[H+]` but have not yet done equilibrium.

## Learning objective
By the end, students can explain that **acid strength = extent of dissociation**
(a property of the acid), which is **independent of concentration** (how much is
dissolved), and can predict that a dilute strong acid can have a *higher* pH than a
concentrated weak acid.

## Lesson outline

**1. Hook (1 min).**
"Which is more acidic: a drop of stomach acid (HCl) in a swimming pool, or a bottle
of vinegar? Most people guess the HCl because it's 'stronger.' Let's see why that's
not enough to know."

**2. Define the two ideas (3 min).**
- *Strength* = what fraction of the acid molecules give up their H+ in water.
  - Strong acid (HCl): essentially 100% dissociates. `HCl -> H+ + Cl-` (one arrow).
  - Weak acid (acetic acid): only a small fraction dissociates. `CH3COOH <=> H+ + CH3COO-`
    (double arrow — an equilibrium).
- *Concentration* = how many moles of acid you dissolved per liter. A separate knob.

**3. Worked contrast (3 min).**
- 0.0001 M HCl: fully dissociates -> [H+] = 0.0001 M -> pH = 4.
- 1.0 M acetic acid: only ~0.4% dissociates -> [H+] ≈ 0.004 M -> pH ≈ 2.4.
- Punchline: the *weak* acid solution is more acidic here, because there's so much
  more of it. Strength and concentration are different questions.

**4. Check + close (1 min).**
"If I dilute a strong acid, does it become a weak acid?" (No — strength is a fixed
property; you've just changed concentration.)
