5. Cognition-informed study-skill guides (JG)

Source: email sent in lieu of a recipe card. Name and signature reduced to initials; the rest of the message is verbatim.

Hi Marlon and Jordan,
Thanks for letting me email my recipe idea to you. Here it is…

Recipe Name:
Cognition-informed study-skill guides

Ingredients:
[These are examples; other sources could be used]
How People Learn: Brain, Mind, Experience, and School (NRC, 2000) https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/9853/chapter/1 , especially Chapters 2 and 3
Susan A. Ambrose et al. How Learning Works: Seven Research-Based Principles for Smart Teaching https://ebookcentral-proquest-com.ezp-prod1.hul.harvard.edu/lib/harvard-ebooks/detail.action?docID=529947
Edward Redish (2002). Teaching Physics with the Physics Suite https://web.archive.org/web/20160321020443/http://www2.physics.umd.edu/~redish/Book/, especially Chapters 2 and 3, and somewhat also Chapters 6, 7, 8
https://tophat.com/blog/blooms-taxonomy/
Mayer, R. E. (1992). Cognition and instruction: Their historic meeting within educational psychology. Journal of Educational Psychology, 84(4), 405–412 https://psycnet.apa.org/doiLanding?doi=10.1037%2F0022-0663.84.4.405 (I'm digging for a current link, probably in Hollis)

Instructions:
From a viewpoint of undergraduate-student learning and cognition, summarize the important information and guidance on student learning and study skills for students. Include the historical progression of such guidance, although that is not the primary aspect to focus on.

Serving:
Two parallel guides in the form of a blog post or series of blog posts: one for students to help them learn about their own learning, and one for teachers, tutors, etc., who are coaching students on learning and study skills. Cite/attribute sources appropriately.

And thanks for putting on this workshop!
— JG
