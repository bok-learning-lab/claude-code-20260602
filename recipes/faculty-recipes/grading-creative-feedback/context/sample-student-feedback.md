# Sample student feedback (the thing being graded)

**Feedback author:** Student B (anonymized) · **Responding to:** Student A,
"The Last Good Light"

> *This is the artifact we grade. It has two parts, mirroring the recipe card:
> (1) handwritten "intent" notes the reviewer jotted while reading, transcribed
> here, and (2) the end-of-summary comments they wrote for the author.*

## Part 1 — Intent / margin notes (transcribed from handwriting)

- "the LIST is the whole story — boots, dog, the light. like a ledger of loss"
- "p1 'wrote in pencil so the salt couldn't fade what the salt had already
  done' — say this twice out loud, it's the best line"
- "girl shows up out of nowhere?? do we need to believe her or is fog = dream?"
- "ending: 'not a subtraction' — I get it but had to reread. good or too clever?"
- "the bus-stop simile — pulled me OUT of the lighthouse for a sec"

## Part 2 — End-of-summary comments (written for the author)

> I really love this piece — it's haunting and the lighthouse keeper feels real.
>
> The thing that's working best is the list as a structure. Tracking loss by page
> number ("page two hundred and six… *the good light*") lets you measure grief
> without ever using the word, and the closing reversal — the new entry that's
> "not a subtraction" — pays that structure off beautifully. I had to read the
> last lines twice, but the second time it landed; you might trust the reader a
> hair more there and not worry that it's too quiet.
>
> One craft thing to look at: the simile comparing the boats to "people who don't
> see the old man at the bus stop." The *idea* is good (he's been made invisible),
> but the bus stop yanked me out of the sea world for a beat. Could the comparison
> stay inside the story's own imagery — something coastal — so we don't leave the
> island?
>
> My one bigger question is the girl. Right now she arrives with no setup and I
> wasn't sure whether to read her as literal or as something dreamed out of the
> fog. I don't think you need to explain her, but a single concrete detail
> (something cold and physical about her, the way the boat rope feels) might let
> us commit to her being *real* if that's what you want.
>
> Overall: gorgeous. Keep the list, soften nothing about the grief, just reground
> us once or twice so the magic feels chosen and not accidental.

---

*Note for graders: this feedback is strong on specificity, craft, and tone, but
the actionability is uneven — the "bus stop" note gives a clear direction, while
the praise and the ending note tell the author to "trust the reader" without a
concrete move. Good test case for the rubric.*
