# Key principles — the shared evidence base

Seven evidence-based learning principles, in original prose. Each gives **the
principle**, **why it works** (the cognitive mechanism), and a **citation pointer**
to the source(s) that ground it. Both guides — student-facing and coach-facing —
draw from this same list, so the two stay parallel. See `reading-list.md` for full
references and `CLAUDE.md` for the citation rules (attribute by reference; never
reproduce source text).

---

## 1. Prior knowledge is the foundation — and it can help or hurt

**Principle.** New learning is built on what a person already knows. Accurate prior
knowledge gives new ideas something to attach to; inaccurate or inert prior knowledge
(misconceptions, or facts the learner never connects to the task) actively gets in
the way.

**Why it works.** The mind doesn't store facts in isolation; it links incoming
information to existing structures. When the existing structure is sound, encoding is
faster and recall is more reliable. When it's flawed, the new material is bent to fit
the wrong model — which is why misconceptions are so stubborn and survive ordinary
instruction.

**Grounds in.** *How People Learn* (NRC, 2000), Ch. 2; Ambrose et al. (2010), Ch. 1;
Redish (2003) on student preconceptions in physics.

---

## 2. Knowledge is organized, not listed

**Principle.** Experts don't just know more than novices — they store what they know
in richly connected, meaningful structures. How you organize material as you study
matters as much as how much you cover.

**Why it works.** Connected knowledge is retrievable knowledge: a well-structured
mental model lets you find the right idea by reasoning from related ones, rather than
hunting through an undifferentiated pile of facts. Experts "chunk" around deep
principles; novices around surface features.

**Grounds in.** *How People Learn* (NRC, 2000), Ch. 2 (expert–novice differences);
Ambrose et al. (2010), Ch. 2; Redish (2003) on expert vs. novice knowledge structure.

---

## 3. Retrieval practice beats rereading

**Principle.** Pulling information *out* of memory — self-testing, recalling without
the book, explaining from scratch — produces more durable learning than putting it
*in* again by rereading or highlighting.

**Why it works.** Each act of retrieval strengthens and re-consolidates the memory
trace and the cues that reach it. Rereading feels productive because the material
looks familiar, but familiarity is not the same as the ability to recall it under
exam conditions — a classic illusion of competence.

**Grounds in.** Consistent with the active-learner, practice-and-feedback view in
Ambrose et al. (2010), Ch. 5, and the constructivist account in *How People Learn*
(NRC, 2000); a well-established cognitive-psychology finding (the testing effect).

---

## 4. Space and interleave your practice

**Principle.** The same total study time produces more lasting learning when it is
distributed across days (spacing) and when related topics are mixed rather than
practiced in long single-topic blocks (interleaving).

**Why it works.** Spacing forces partial forgetting between sessions, and the effort
of reloading the material is itself what strengthens it. Interleaving forces the
learner to choose *which* approach a problem needs, not just execute a method already
primed by the previous problem — building the discrimination skills real performance
requires.

**Grounds in.** Practice-and-feedback principle in Ambrose et al. (2010), Ch. 5; the
active-processing model in *How People Learn* (NRC, 2000); established distributed-
practice research.

---

## 5. Metacognition: monitor and adjust your own learning

**Principle.** Strong learners plan, monitor whether their study is actually working,
and change strategy when it isn't. This self-direction is learnable, not a fixed
trait.

**Why it works.** Learning is only efficient if you can tell the difference between
"I recognize this" and "I can produce this." Accurate self-monitoring catches the
familiarity illusion early and reallocates effort to what isn't yet solid; poor
monitoring leaves learners confidently under-prepared.

**Grounds in.** *How People Learn* (NRC, 2000), Ch. 2 (metacognition); Ambrose et al.
(2010), Ch. 7 (self-directed learners); Redish (2003) on students reflecting on their
own reasoning.

---

## 6. Motivation, value, and expectancy drive effort

**Principle.** People invest effort in learning when they see the work as *valuable*
and believe they can *succeed* at it. Both beliefs have to be present; either one at
zero shuts effort down.

**Why it works.** Goal-directed behavior is gated by expected payoff. A task seen as
pointless gets no effort even if it's easy; a task seen as hopeless gets none even if
it matters. A supportive climate and early, achievable wins move both levers.

**Grounds in.** Ambrose et al. (2010), Ch. 3 (motivation) and Ch. 6 (climate);
constructivist learner-as-active-agent framing in Mayer (1992).

---

## 7. Study (and teach) at the level you'll be asked to perform — transfer

**Principle.** Match the cognitive demand of your practice to the demand of the real
task. If you'll be asked to *apply* or *analyze*, practicing only *recall* leaves you
unprepared. Knowledge that's tied to one narrow context often fails to transfer to a
new one.

**Why it works.** Memory is cue-dependent and context-sensitive. Practicing a skill
at a low cognitive level, or only in one setting, builds a representation that the
new, harder, or differently-framed task can't reach. Practicing at the target level —
and across varied contexts — builds the flexible, abstracted understanding that
transfers. Bloom's hierarchy gives a shared vocabulary for naming the level.

**Grounds in.** Bloom et al. (1956); revised Anderson & Krathwohl (2001); transfer
and context-dependence in *How People Learn* (NRC, 2000), Ch. 3; Redish (2003) on
context-dependence of knowledge.

## Secondary thread — historical progression

A brief through-line both guides can use as framing: study advice moved from the
**behaviorist** era (drill, reinforcement, observable performance), through the
**information-processing cognitivist** turn (memory, encoding, mental
representations), toward a **constructivist** view of the learner as an active
sense-maker who builds understanding on prior knowledge. Mayer (1992) frames this as
the "historic meeting" of cognition and instruction; it explains *why* today's
advice emphasizes active retrieval, organization, and metacognition rather than
passive repetition. Keep this secondary — a paragraph of context, not the spine.
