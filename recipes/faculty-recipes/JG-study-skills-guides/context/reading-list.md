# Reading list — the shared evidence base

The source list JG supplied (these are examples; other sources could be substituted).
Each entry gives a full reference, a link, a short **reference key** used throughout
this project, and a one-line note on what it contributes. Both guides cite from this
list; neither reproduces copyrighted text from these works.

---

## 1. How People Learn

**Reference:** National Research Council. (2000). *How People Learn: Brain, Mind,
Experience, and School* (Expanded Edition). Washington, DC: National Academies Press.
(Especially Chapters 2 and 3.)
**Link:** https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/9853/chapter/1
**Reference key:** *How People Learn* (NRC, 2000)
**Contributes:** The foundational synthesis — expert vs. novice knowledge
organization, the central role of prior knowledge, and metacognition as a learnable
skill. Anchors the "knowledge is organized, not listed" and "monitor your own
understanding" principles.

## 2. How Learning Works

**Reference:** Ambrose, S. A., Bridges, M. W., DiPietro, M., Lovett, M. C., & Norman,
M. K. (2010). *How Learning Works: Seven Research-Based Principles for Smart
Teaching.* San Francisco: Jossey-Bass.
**Link:** Harvard ebook access via ProQuest Ebook Central (search the title in
HOLLIS / library e-resources).
**Reference key:** Ambrose et al. (2010)
**Contributes:** Seven actionable principles bridging research and classroom practice
— prior knowledge, knowledge organization, motivation, mastery/component skills,
practice + feedback, student development/climate, and self-directed (metacognitive)
learning. The most directly "translatable" source for both guides.

## 3. Teaching Physics with the Physics Suite

**Reference:** Redish, E. F. (2003). *Teaching Physics with the Physics Suite.*
Hoboken, NJ: John Wiley & Sons. (Especially Chapters 2 and 3; also relevant: 6, 7, 8.
JG cited the 2002 manuscript edition.)
**Link:** https://web.archive.org/web/20160321020443/http://www2.physics.umd.edu/~redish/Book/
**Reference key:** Redish (2003)
**Contributes:** A discipline-grounded cognitive model of learning — mental models,
the role of pre-existing student conceptions (and misconceptions), context-dependence
of knowledge, and why naive ideas resist instruction. Grounds the prior-knowledge and
transfer principles in a STEM setting that matches JG's discipline.

## 4. Bloom's taxonomy

**Reference:** Bloom, B. S. (Ed.). (1956). *Taxonomy of Educational Objectives:
The Classification of Educational Goals. Handbook I: Cognitive Domain.* New York:
Longmans, Green. Revised in: Anderson, L. W., & Krathwohl, D. R. (Eds.). (2001).
*A Taxonomy for Learning, Teaching, and Assessing.* New York: Longman.
**Link (overview):** https://tophat.com/blog/blooms-taxonomy/
**Reference key:** Bloom et al. (1956); revised Anderson & Krathwohl (2001)
**Contributes:** A hierarchy of cognitive demand (remember → understand → apply →
analyze → evaluate → create) that lets learners and coaches name the *level* a task
asks for, and recognize the gap between recognizing material and using it. Grounds the
"study at the level you'll be tested at" idea and the historical-progression thread.

## 5. Mayer (1992) — the historic meeting

**Reference:** Mayer, R. E. (1992). Cognition and instruction: Their historic meeting
within educational psychology. *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 84(4), 405–412.
**Link:** https://psycnet.apa.org/doiLanding?doi=10.1037%2F0022-0663.84.4.405
**Reference key:** Mayer (1992)
**Contributes:** The historical spine (secondary thread) — how the field moved from
behaviorism, through information-processing cognitivism, toward constructivist views
of the learner as an active sense-maker. Frames *why* modern study advice looks the
way it does.

## How to cite in the guides

Use the short reference key inline, e.g. "(Ambrose et al., 2010)" or "as summarized
in *How People Learn* (NRC, 2000)". Each guide ends with a **Sources** section
listing the full references above. Attribute principles to their grounding source(s);
never paste sentences from the originals.
