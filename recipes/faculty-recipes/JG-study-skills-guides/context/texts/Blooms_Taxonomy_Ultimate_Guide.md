## 1. What is Bloom's taxonomy?

Bloom's taxonomy is a framework for learning, teaching and educational achievement in which each level depends on the one below. It's often depicted in the form of a pyramid—similar to Maslow's hierarchy of needs.

Basic knowledge, the first stage of learning, leads to the development of the skills and abilities that are crucial to completing the pedagogical process: Comprehension, application, analysis, synthesis and evaluation. While there are subcategories within each, each stage lies on a continuum. The belief is that students move up through each level of the pyramid in Bloom's taxonomy, starting from very basic learning, to acquire deeper knowledge on a subject, with each level crucial to the development of the next.

Teachers can apply Bloom's taxonomy by asking questions and delivering assignments that directly correlate with specific learning objectives in each stage of the process, making the objectives clear to the student. For example, posing multiple-choice questions can help gauge a student's level of basic understanding and remembering of a subject, while asking a student to come up with a comparison or analogy points towards entering the application or analysis stage.

## 2. The history of Bloom's taxonomy

### 2.1. Original Bloom's taxonomy from 1956

In the 1940s, Benjamin Bloom, along with his collaborators Max Englehart, Edward Furst, Walter Hill and David Krathwohl, devised Bloom's taxonomy to place educational goals into specific categories, with the belief that this classification would be useful to better assess college student performance.

Each year for the following 16 years, Bloom and his colleagues revised and refined the framework at the American Psychological Association convention. In 1956, the final version was published as the *Taxonomy of Educational Objectives*, showing the path of educational attainment through six orders of learning.

> "After forty years of intensive research on school learning in the United States as well as abroad, my major conclusion is: What any person in the world can learn, almost all persons can learn if provided with appropriate prior and current conditions of learning."
> — Benjamin Bloom

The original taxonomy has served as the backbone of many teaching philosophies ever since. While it initially aided in the assessment of students, it quickly became a tool for teachers to devise their curriculum, outline clear learning objectives, and design classroom activities. It has been adapted for use in classrooms from K–12 to college and at the university level.

**Key takeaways:**
- Benjamin Bloom and several of his peers developed Bloom's Taxonomy in 1956 to better assess college student performance.
- Bloom's Taxonomy consists of six different levels of learning that build on one another to guide students and educators through the stage of educational attainment.

### 2.2. Revised Bloom's taxonomy from 2001

In 2001, a group of cognitive psychologists, curriculum theorists, instructional researchers and testing assessment specialists led by Lorin Anderson, a colleague of Krathwohl's and former student of Bloom's, aimed to reorganize and create a revised Bloom's taxonomy. This involved putting together a series of more dynamic concepts for the classification system as compared to the original static, one-dimensional levels of educational objectives.

At the core of the revision of Bloom's taxonomy is the use of verbs to replace nouns—providing learners with clearer objectives for what is expected of them.

| Older Version | Revised Bloom's Taxonomy |
|---------------|--------------------------|
| Knowledge     | Remember                 |
| Comprehension | Understand               |
| Application   | Apply                    |
| Analysis      | Analyze                  |
| Synthesis     | Evaluate                 |
| Evaluation    | Create                   |

The new revision swaps the two final Bloom's taxonomy levels of learning, Synthesis/Evaluation, making them clearer and emphasizing the application of knowledge, which is the end goal of effective learning.

Additionally, Bloom's revised taxonomy separates the cognitive domain into four distinct types within a matrix:

- **Factual**: Characterized by terminology and discrete facts.
- **Conceptual**: Categories, principles, theories, and models—looking at the relationships among all elements within a larger structure.
- **Procedural**: The knowledge of a specific technique, process, or methodology—essentially, how to do something.
- **Metacognitive**: A student's self-assessment of their ability and knowledge of different skills and techniques.

**Key takeaways:**
- Revised Bloom's Taxonomy focuses on the use of verbs and action words to provide learners with a clearer understanding of what is expected of them.
- Bloom's revised taxonomy separates the cognitive domain into four distinct types within a matrix: factual, conceptual, procedural and metacognitive.

## 3. Why is Bloom's taxonomy important?

Bloom's taxonomy has been actively used by teachers from K-12 to college instructors for over five decades. Yet it is still just as important today as it was in the 1950s.

At the heart of Bloom's taxonomy framework is the ability to create achievable learning goals that teachers and students understand, and build a definitive plan to meet them. Instructors are encouraged to view learning objectives in behavioral terms, such that they can see what students are capable of as a direct result of the instruction they have received at each level, without the need for class-wide generalizations.

Using the categorization, educators can more effectively organize objectives and create lesson plans with appropriate content and instruction to lead students up the pyramid of learning. Educators can also design valid assessment tools and strategies to ensure each category is met in turn, and that each part of the course material is in line with the level's objectives.

At the end of the learning process, the goal with Bloom's taxonomy is that a student has honed a new skill, level of knowledge, and/or developed a different attitude towards the subject. And that teachers can effectively assess this learning on an ongoing basis, as the course moves through each stage of the framework.

**Key takeaways:**
- Bloom's Taxonomy is essential because it helps educators identify achievable learning goals and develop plans to meet them.
- The Bloom's Taxonomy framework allows educators to assess learning on an ongoing basis, encouraging students to reflect on their progress.

## 4. The levels of thinking in Bloom's taxonomy

The revised Bloom's taxonomy framework starts with the most basic level of knowledge at the bottom, **Remembering**, whereby students recall facts and basic concepts, and moves up towards the pinnacle: **Create**, where new or original work is produced.

### 4.1. What the levels of thinking in Bloom's taxonomy mean

In any learning environment, it's critical to start from the bottom level and work your way up. The lower-order skills require less cognitive process but provide an important base for learning. Meanwhile, the higher Bloom's levels require deeper learning and a greater degree of cognitive processing, which can only be achieved once the lower-order skills have been mastered.

**Key takeaway:** Bloom's Taxonomy is a hierarchical framework that encourages learners to work their way up towards higher-order thinking and cognitive tasks.

### 4.2. How to use the Bloom's taxonomy levels of thinking

Each level of Bloom's taxonomy should be addressed before moving on to the next. When course planning, bear in mind the implications—how quickly to introduce new concepts, when to reinforce them and how to test them.

Use three key pillars to achieve clear learning outcomes:
- **Condition**: The resource being used
- **Performance**: What students should accomplish by the end
- **Criteria**: The method of measuring success

Importantly, some education-related words like *include*, *understand* and *learn* can't be measured in a meaningful way. Following Bloom's taxonomy makes performance actionable and effective, using verbs that set clear expectations that can be specifically measured.

### 4.3. Bloom's taxonomy Level 1: Remembering

In the first stage, students recite information from memory based on previous lectures, reading material, and notes. Key verbs: **define, describe, identify, label, list, outline, recall, reproduce**.

It's the most basic level in Bloom's taxonomy, but represents an important foundation. A basic way to test learning is simple Q&A or multiple-choice questions—this shows the student can memorize and recall facts, though it does not yet prove comprehension.

**Key takeaway:** The first level of Bloom's Taxonomy helps build a solid foundation and acts as a stepping stone towards more complex learning.

### 4.4. Bloom's taxonomy Level 2: Understanding

Students discuss a problem or idea in their own words to evaluate their comprehension. They might paraphrase a story, explain a concept, tell a related story, or provide analogies. Key verbs: **defend, explain, generalize, paraphrase, summarize, translate**.

**Key takeaway:** This level asks students to explain course concepts in their own words.

### 4.5. Bloom's taxonomy Level 3: Applying

Students take what they've learned and apply it to a scenario outside of the classroom—using a math formula to calculate a real-world budget, or applying a legal ruling to a news case. Key verbs: **apply, demonstrate, predict, show, solve, use**.

**Key takeaway:** This level encourages students to extend their learning outside the classroom by finding similarities and differences in the real world.

### 4.6. Bloom's taxonomy Level 4: Analyzing

Students draw connections between ideas, utilize critical thinking, and break down knowledge into its component parts. Examples include using logical deduction to figure out how equipment works, or finding fallacies in an argument. Key verbs: **analyze, break down, compare, contrast, differentiate, deconstruct, infer**.

**Key takeaway:** This level allows students to use their critical thinking skills to understand how or why different concepts work together.

### 4.7. Bloom's taxonomy Level 5: Evaluating

Students make educated judgments about the value of material they've learned—finding effective solutions to problems or justifying decisions with knowledge. Key verbs: **appraise, conclude, critique, evaluate, support, summarize**. Tools like surveys and blogs can help at this level.

**Key takeaway:** This level asks students to make value judgments about the material they've learned.

### 4.8. Bloom's taxonomy Level 6: Creating

In the final level, students demonstrate full knowledge by applying what they've learned to build something tangible or conceptual—writing a manual, designing machinery, or revising a process. Key verbs: **categorize, combine, compile, devise, design, generate, modify, write**.

**Key takeaway:** This level encourages students to demonstrate their knowledge by building something tangible or conceptual.

## 5. What are the Bloom's taxonomy objectives?

Bloom's taxonomy is divided into three distinct domains of educational activities: **cognitive**, **affective**, and **psychomotor**. These are also referred to by the acronym **KSA**:
- **K**nowledge (cognitive)
- **S**kills (psychomotor)
- **A**ttitudes (affective)

The goal is that by the end of a learning session, the student will have acquired new knowledge, skills and attitudes towards a subject.

### 5.1. The cognitive domain in Bloom's taxonomy

Knowledge and development of intellectual skills is at the heart of the cognitive domain, whereby a student can recall or recognize facts, patterns, and concepts that will serve as a foundation for deeper learning. This is where the six key facets of Bloom's taxonomy—Knowledge, Comprehension, Application, Analysis, Synthesis and Evaluation—come in.

### 5.2. The affective domain in Bloom's taxonomy

In this domain, students develop new feelings or emotions about the subject and/or themselves. They should be able to place more value on something, have a greater appreciation for it, along with different motivations and attitudes. Students can be assessed on their ability to:
- Listen with respect and provide undivided attention
- Actively participate in class discussions
- Resolve conflicts
- Exhibit consistent behaviors that reflect their internalized values

### 5.3. The psychomotor domain in Bloom's taxonomy

The psychomotor domain was one of the later additions to Bloom's taxonomy, as the original team did not believe they had sufficient knowledge in teaching such skills at the post-secondary level. In this domain, students develop manual or physical skills across three versions: physical movement, coordination and the use of motor skills.

Examples include: properly stitching a wound (medical), operating a backhoe (construction), or following choreographed dance steps. Psychomotor skills are measured in terms of **speed, precision, distance, procedures and technique**.

**Key takeaways:**
- Bloom's taxonomy consists of three domains: cognitive, psychomotor and affective.
- Instructors are encouraged to design learning plans so that students acquire new knowledge, skills and attitudes towards a subject.

## 6. How to use Bloom's taxonomy in the classroom + Bloom's taxonomy examples

### 6.1. Using Bloom's taxonomy in lesson planning and course design

Educators can use Bloom's taxonomy to precisely focus curricula throughout the year on specific parts of the framework, ensuring that students demonstrate the proper cognitive abilities in each assignment and exam before moving on to the next.

Students can have clear, concise, and measurable goals to achieve. Questions using verbs like *compare*, *discuss*, and *predict* help basic understanding, while verbs like *investigate* and *relate* suggest movement into the analyzing stage.

**Key takeaway:** Educators should design lesson and curriculum plans following Bloom's Taxonomy so students can build on their learning and progress through the levels throughout the term.

### 6.2. Bloom's taxonomy and active learning

Bloom's taxonomy fits extremely well into lesson planning for active learning:

- In the **Remember** stage: ask students to challenge each other to recollect facts, or list the most important facts learned that day.
- In the **Analyze** stage: spark class discussions by exploring problems, comparisons, and examining how a subject relates to students' everyday lives.

Being explicit about expectations also helps guide students through metacognition within Bloom's taxonomy, encouraging them to take responsibility for their own learning.

**Key takeaway:** Each of Bloom's Taxonomy levels is designed with active learning in mind, giving students a sense of responsibility for their learning.

### 6.3. Bloom's taxonomy and formative assessment

Formative assessment doesn't directly impact a student's grade, but it helps educators gauge how well students are learning and moving up the Bloom's taxonomy hierarchy.

- Focus on what you want students to achieve, using Bloom's taxonomy as a guide.
- Develop concrete learning objectives for each stage and give clear expectations.
- Match assessment techniques and questions to the lecture.

In the **Remember and Understand** stage (entry-level), multiple-choice or true/false questions work well. At the top **Analyze, Evaluate, and Create** levels, consider oral examinations or written essays.

**Key takeaway:** Bloom's taxonomy can be used to test and reinforce learning as the term progresses, allowing educators to course-correct where students are struggling.

### 6.4. Bloom's taxonomy and summative assessment

For graded assignments and exams:
- **Mid-term exams**: typically cover material closer to the bottom of the pyramid (Remembering, Understanding, Applying).
- **Final exams**: assess learning towards the top (Analyzing, Evaluating, Creating).

By the summative assessment, students should be able to apply knowledge to everyday situations, provide and defend informed opinions, and consider additional questions. Creative formats like booklets, mock marketing campaigns, flowcharts, or tip sheets can effectively measure top-level Bloom's learning.

**Key takeaway:** Bloom's Taxonomy allows students to apply their knowledge in creative ways, enabling flexible assessments that let students demonstrate their learning meaningfully.

### 6.5. Bloom's taxonomy for STEM Classes

With much emphasis on meeting math and science standards—particularly in introductory courses—higher-order thinking skills are sometimes deprioritized. Most STEM assessments consist of multiple-choice questions focusing on the lower levels of Bloom's Taxonomy, but much of what students need for success outside the classroom requires them to proficiently apply, analyze, synthesize, and evaluate information.

The good news is that STEM subjects lend themselves well to Bloom's Taxonomy's higher levels. Educators can help students internalize course concepts by designing engaging activities with higher-order question stems. Students will not only perform better on assessments, but also be better prepared to apply their learnings outside the classroom.

Bloom's revised taxonomy gives educators the ability to construct a curriculum to assess objective learning outcomes. Pre-created Bloom's taxonomy question stems make engaging students in each level easier, allowing educators to plan opportunities for students to learn, reflect and assess their learning in motivating and creative ways throughout the term.

## 8. Problems with Bloom's taxonomy

Bloom's taxonomy is by no means a hard and fast rule book; it's a theoretical construct that can be interpreted in many ways to fit individual teaching styles, courses, and lesson plans. Some believe it is only appropriate for the lower levels of learning and that it fails to address more recent developments in cognitive psychology. Others question the requirement that students must start at the lowest level before engaging in meaningful dialog about facts.

### 8.1. Creativity as a goal, not as a tool

Sometimes, creativity isn't just a goal—it's a tool that can be effectively used to further learning. Educators could ask students to create something in the first lesson (e.g., a mock advertisement or a proposed solution to global warming), then deconstruct and compare results to introduce facts, concepts, and basic knowledge.

In this respect, Bloom's taxonomy structure can morph into various forms—a circle, a web, a flower, or even a mandala—showing each level of learning feeding into one another at different points in the process. No matter how it's organized, Bloom's taxonomy always uses the six key principles to result in deeper learning.

### 8.2. Over-reliance

With Bloom's taxonomy, the 21st-century revision proved further refinement was necessary to keep the framework relevant. Sticking to the template without thinking about the reasons behind it can lead to over-reliance on a literal interpretation.

For example, just because a student can defend a position doesn't mean they're doing so in more than a superficial way. And the ability to devise a detailed plan isn't evidence that the plan reflects good judgment and analysis. Using Bloom's taxonomy as a *guide*—ensuring all six levels of learning are covered in whatever way works best—can put educators on the right path to success.
