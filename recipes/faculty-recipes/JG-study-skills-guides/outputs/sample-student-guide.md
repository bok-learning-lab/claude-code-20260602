# Learning About Your Own Learning: A Student's Field Guide (excerpt)

You already know how to study — you've been doing it for years. The question this
guide asks is sharper: *does the way you study actually match how your brain learns?*
A lot of common study habits feel productive while doing surprisingly little. Here are
two places where a small change pays off a lot.

## 1. Start from what you already know

When you open a new chapter, your mind doesn't file it away on a blank shelf. It hooks
the new material onto whatever is already up there. That's why a topic clicks fast
when you have good background — and why it stays confusing when your background is
thin or, worse, slightly wrong (*How People Learn*, NRC, 2000; Ambrose et al., 2010).

**What to do this week:**
- Before you read or attend a lecture, spend two minutes writing down what you already
  think you know about the topic — even rough guesses. You're giving the new material
  hooks to attach to.
- When something refuses to make sense, suspect a *wrong* hook, not a missing one. Ask:
  "what do I currently believe about this that the new material is contradicting?"
  Naming a misconception is most of the work of fixing it.

**How to know it's working:** if you can connect today's idea to something from last
week (or from another course), the hook held. If it floats free, go build the
background first.

## 2. Test yourself — don't just reread

Here's the most reliable upgrade in this whole guide: **practice pulling the material
out of your memory instead of pushing it back in.** Rereading and highlighting feel
great because the page looks familiar — but familiarity is a trap. Recognizing
something on the page is not the same as being able to produce it on an exam. Every
time you *retrieve* an idea from memory, you make it easier to retrieve next time
(*How People Learn*, NRC, 2000; Ambrose et al., 2010 — the testing effect).

**What to do this week:**
- Close the book and write down everything you can recall about a topic from memory.
  *Then* open it and check what you missed. The gaps are your real study list.
- Turn headings and figures into questions and answer them out loud before you look.
- Explain the idea to a friend (or an empty room) without notes. If you can't, you've
  found the soft spot — and you found it before the exam did.

**How to know it's working:** the goal isn't a clean, comfortable review session. A
little struggle to recall is the sign that learning is actually happening.
