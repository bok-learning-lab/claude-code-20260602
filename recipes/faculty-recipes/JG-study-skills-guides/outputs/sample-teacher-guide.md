# Coaching Students on How They Learn: A Tutor's Field Guide (excerpt)

Most students don't have a study-skills problem so much as a *feedback* problem — they
can't tell which of their habits are working. Your leverage as a coach is to make the
invisible visible and hand the steering back to them. Here are the coach's moves for
two high-payoff principles. (Each maps directly onto a section of the student guide.)

## 1. Start from what they already know

**Why it matters.** Students build new understanding on existing structures. Accurate
prior knowledge accelerates learning; inaccurate or inert prior knowledge quietly
derails it, and misconceptions survive ordinary instruction (*How People Learn*, NRC,
2000; Ambrose et al., 2010; Redish, 2003, on stubborn student preconceptions in STEM).

**Diagnostics — how to spot it.**
- Ask the student to explain the topic *before* you teach it. Listen for confident
  wrong models, not just gaps — the confident ones are the dangerous ones.
- When a student is stuck, probe whether the obstacle is a *missing* prerequisite or a
  *conflicting* belief. They present very differently and need different fixes.

**Coaching moves.**
- Open sessions with a low-stakes "what do you already think about X?" — it surfaces
  hooks (and misconceptions) and primes the student to connect new material.
- When you find a misconception, don't just overwrite it; make the student articulate
  it and then test it against a case where it fails. Confrontation, not correction,
  is what dislodges it.
- *(Student's side: "Start from what you already know.")*

## 2. Push them toward retrieval, not rereading

**Why it matters.** Retrieving information strengthens memory far more than re-exposing
the student to it. But rereading produces a feeling of fluency that students mistake
for mastery — so they under-study confidently (*How People Learn*, NRC, 2000; Ambrose
et al., 2010, on practice and feedback; the testing effect).

**Diagnostics — how to spot it.**
- Ask "how do you study?" If the answer is rereading, highlighting, or recopying
  notes, you've found it.
- Have them rate their confidence on a topic, *then* ask them to explain it from
  memory. A large confidence-performance gap is the familiarity illusion in action —
  and it's persuasive to show them their own gap.

**Coaching moves.**
- Replace "review the chapter" with "close the book and tell me what you remember,
  then we'll find the holes together." Make retrieval the default activity in session.
- Assign self-testing between sessions (recall, self-quizzing, teaching it to someone)
  rather than rereading; check in on the gaps it exposes.
- Normalize the difficulty: tell them the struggle to recall *is* the learning, so
  they don't abandon the harder method for the comfortable one.
- *(Student's side: "Test yourself — don't just reread.")*
